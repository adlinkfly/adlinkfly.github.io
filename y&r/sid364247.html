DEFINITIONS
Spondylolysis is an interruption of the pars interarticularis, which may be either unilateral or bilateral. The term spondylolysis is derived from two Greek roots: spondylos, meaning “vertebra,” and lysis, meaning a “dissolution, loosening, setting free, or releasing.” (Fig. 5-1)
Spondylolisthesis is traditionally defined as an anterior displacement of a vertebral body in relation to the segment immediately below. The Greek origin of this word is spondylos, meaning “vertebra,” and listhesis, which refers to “slippage or displacement” but without regard for direction. (Fig. 5-1) Traditionally, and even today, the term spondylolisthesis in medical literature is synonymous with forward displacement. Unfortunately, this is an erroneous use of the word. While a more precise term for this anatomic disrelationship would be “anterolisthesis with or without spondylolysis,” we will yield to convention in this chapter and use spondylolisthesis to mean anterior vertebral displacement, unless otherwise indicated. Spondylolisthesis (anterolisthesis) may occur with a defect in the vertebral arch (spondylolytic spondylolisthesis) or without a defect in the vertebral arch (non-spondylolytic spondylolisthesis). (1)
The term prespondylolisthesis has been used to indicate the presence of spondylolysis without forward vertebral displacement. This term is misleading and should be discarded because it erroneously implies that displacement will necessarily occur.
HISTORICAL CONSIDERATIONS
Spondylolisthesis has been a focal point of academic and clinical interest for more than 100 years, and still many questions remain unanswered in regard to the cause, clinical significance, treatment, and prognosis of this common condition.
Defects in the pars interarticularis have been described in skeletons dating back to 1000-6000 B.C. (1) The first recorded description of spondylolisthesis was written in 1782 by the Belgian obstetrician Herbinaux. (2) The term spondylolisthesis was introduced by Kilian (3), who concluded that these displacements occurred gradually. Herbinaux’s original report was of a patient who experienced difficulty in childbirth because of displacement of the L5 vertebra relative to the S1 segment. This condition stimulated the interest of obstetricians, who identified it as a cause of obstructed labor. It is doubtful that the spondylolisthesis had anything to do with the patient’s difficulty with delivery. Wiltse has never observed difficulty with delivery, no matter how severe the displacement. (LL Wiltse, personal communication, 1990)
In 1855, Robert (4) performed experiments on cadavers in which he removed all soft tissues around suspected anterolisthesis at the lumbosacral junction, exposing the pars interarticularis. His studies revealed that no displacement occurred if the neural arch was intact, an observation later challenged. (5,6) In 1858, Lambl (5) demonstrated the pars interarticularis defect that is known as spondylolysis. In 1895, Neugebauer (6) was the first to describe

displacement of the L5 vertebral body on the sacrum that occurred without pars interarticularis defects as a result of congenital elongation of the pars interarticularis (type I: dysplastic).

GRADING SYSTEMS OF ANTEROLISTHESIS
Identifying anterior slippage of a vertebra requires the use of imaging and when identified must subsequently be quantified. When the displacement is large, it is often readily observable, but in many cases malposition can be subtle. To identify displacement, close attention to alignment landmarks through the use of two lines has been used. Most commonly a comparison of the posterior vertebral body lines (George’s line) of L5 and S1 is used, but this method is prone to error. A second method, Ullmann’s line, is more reliable. Ullmann’s line is drawn perpendicular to the sacral base from the anterior sacral promontory; if it intersects through the anteroinferior margin of the L5 vertebral body, an anterolisthesis should be suspected. (Fig. 5-2)
Quantification of anterior displacements are commonly performed by three methods: the Meyerding method, calculation of a percentage, and the millimetric distances method. (1) Although described here for an L5 spondylolisthesis, these mensuration methods can be applied to any lumbar level.
Meyerding Method.
The sacral base length is divided by three lines into four quarters, and the posterior aspect of the displaced vertebra is compared to this division and graded. (2) (Figs. 5-3 and 5-4) In grade 1 spondylolisthesis the posterior L5 vertebral body edge lies between the posterior aspect of the sacrum and the first division; in grade 2 spondylolisthesis it lies between the first and second divisions. In grade 3 spondylolisthesis the posterior edge is stationed between the second and the third divisions, whereas in grade 4 it is positioned between the third division and the anterior sacral promontory. A grade 5 spondylolisthesis (spondyloptosis), although not originally part of the

Meyerding classification, is a relatively rare entity in which the entire L5 vertebral body is dislocated beyond the anterior sacral promontory. (3) (Fig. 5-5)

Percentage Method (of Taillard).
The displacement between the posterior sacral base and the posterior aspect of the L5 vertebra is measured along a plane paralleling the disc in millimeters. The measured displacement (numerator) is then divided by the length of the sacral promontory (denominator) and multiplied by 100. (1,4) The main advantage of this method is the removal of any geometrical magnification.
Millimetric Mensuration.
The absolute distance of slip can be quantified in millimeters by mensuration of the distance between the posterior L5 vertebral body and the posterior sacrum. The measurement is taken in a plane that parallels the plane of the disc. This method does not allow comparison between studies at different film-focal distances and is not as accurate.
CLASSIFICATION OF SPONDYLOLISTHESIS
The most widely used classification of spondylolisthesis is that described by Wiltse et al., who identified five distinct types. (1) Recently, a sixth category has been suggested as a result of the increasing use of surgery in spinal care.
  • Type I: dysplastic. Includes spondylolistheses with a congenital abnormality affecting the upper sacrum or the neural arch of L5 that allows displacement to occur.
  • Type II: isthmic. Has three subtypes that involve direct alteration to the pars interarticularis as follows:
    A lytic or stress (fatigue) fracture of the pars.
    An elongated but intact pars.
    An acute fracture of the pars (rare).
  • Type III: degenerative (pseudo-spondylolisthesis). Is secondary to long-standing degenerative arthrosis of the lumbar zygapophyseal joints and discovertebral articulations, without a pars separation.
  • Type IV: traumatic. Is secondary to a fracture of part of the neural arch other than the pars interarticularis.
  • Type V: pathologic. Occurs in conjunction with generalized or localized bone disease (e.g., Paget’s disease, metastatic bone disease, osteopetrosis).
  • Type VI: iatrogenic. Describes spondylolysis secondary to surgical intervention of the spine.

Type I spondylolisthesis involves a congenital malformation of the upper sacrum and/or the neural arch of the L5 vertebra (1) and is one of the non-spondylolytic (no pars defects) forms of spondylolisthesis. ISTHMIC (TYPE II) SPONDYLOLISTHESIS
Subtype A.
Type IIA spondylolisthesis is caused by biomechanical stress; the mode of failure is believed to be fatigue fracture of the pars interarticularis. It is the most common type found in individuals < 50 years of age but has not been noted in the newborn. (1)
Subtype B.
Type IIB spondylolisthesis results from elongation of the pars interarticularis without separation. It is fundamentally the same lesion as subtype A; however, it is thought to be secondary to repeated, minor trabecular stress fractures of the pars with subsequent healing before complete separation. Pars elongation occurs during the periods of fatigue as the vertebral body of L5 is displaced anteriorly. Eventually, the pars may separate as displacement progresses, making differentiation from subtype A difficult. (1)
Subtype C.
Type IIC spondylolistheses, rarely seen, result from acute pars fractures. The most common mechanism of injury is lower lumbar hyperextension. Displacement seldom occurs. (2) An acute pars fracture without concomitant fracture in other parts of the vertebra (usually a compression fracture of the vertebral body) is seldom identified. (Fig. 5-7) Furthermore, certain disease

Cause
Congenital
For many years isthmic spondylolysis and spondylolisthesis have been referred to as a congenital anomaly (4,5); however, extensive studies of cadavers have not revealed these lesions in the newborn. In 1939 Batts (6) studied 200 fetal spines and found no pars interarticularis defects, nor did Rowe and Roche (7), who examined 509 stillborn and neonatal cadavers. Fredrickson et al. (8) reviewed the lumbar radiographs of 500 newborns in 1984 and found no evidence of spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis, thus supporting an acquired cause. The youngest patient reported with pars defects is a 4-month-old who was born after an uneventful pregnancy and delivery. (4) Borkow and Kleiger (4) reported that shortly after birth the parents noted a deformity in the child’s lower back and brought it to the attention of their physician. Radiologic examination revealed a pars defect at the L4 vertebra and a hypoplastic L5 vertebral body. The youngest patients previously reported were 10 and 17 months of age. (9,10) (Fig. 5-9)
Some authors attempt to explain spondylolysis as a result of non-union between two separate ossification centers in the pars interarticularis or secondary to a defect in cartilaginous development; however, no evidence has been produced to support these theories. (11) It should be understood that the pars is a fully ossified structure at birth. (12,13)
Figure 5-9 SPONDYLOLISTHESIS IN A 3-YEAR-OLD. Lateral Lumbar Spine. Pars defects are visible at the L5 segment (arrow), with accompanying anterolisthesis. (Courtesy of Victor Y. W. Tong, DC, DACBR, Los Angeles, California.)
Although isthmic spondylolysis is not currently considered to be of congenital origin, there is an increased incidence among some families (14) and certain ethnic groups, such as Alaskan Eskimos and other Native Americans. (15) This suggests that there are some genetic factors that may predispose an individual to develop the defect. If the defect is ultimately an acquired lesion of mechanical origin, the amount and distribution of cortical bone within the pars interarticularis must be considered important. (16) The presence of a thin pars interarticularis may be the result of genetic factors. The high familial incidence of this condition may be explained by a thin pars interarticularis, causing otherwise normal physiologic forces on the neural arch to produce critical tensile stress and fatigue fracture at the pars level. Therefore, those born with a thin pars may be predisposed to but not born with spondylolysis.
Several studies have suggested that there are variations in the prevalence of spondylolysis in different populations. There is a 5-7% prevalence of pars defects in whites, with a 2:1 male predominance. (17) Rowe and Roche (7) report an incidence of spondylolisthesis of 2.4% in the black populations of North America and South Africa. Moreton (18) found a 7.2% prevalence of spondylolysis in a pre-employment study of 32,600 asymptomatic white young men aged 18-35 years. In 1953 Stewart (15) studied the skeletal remains of 243 Alaskan Eskimos and found a 40.3% prevalence of pars defects in the specimens. Kettelkamp and Wright (19) reviewed the radiographs of 153 Alaskan Eskimos and noted the presence of pars defects in 28%.

According to Wiltse (11), there is a 13-fold increase in the prevalence of spina bifida occulta among persons with pars defects compared to the general population. (20,21) Spina bifida occulta is most common at the L5 segment and estimated to be present in up to 6% of spines. (7,22) Spina bifida occulta is not necessarily found at the level of the spondylolysis, nor are the disorders etiologically related. (11) According to Janse (23) patients with spina bifida occulta often have hypoplastic and asymmetric facets at the spondylolisthetic segment. This may cause aberrant motion and excessive stress in the neural arch, which may then predispose these patients to develop pars defects. Other posterior arch clefts have been described in addition to pars defects. (24,25) (Fig. 5-11)
  • Persistent neurocentral synchondrosis. In this developmental defect there is failure of fusion between the pedicle and the vertebral body. It is often associated with hypoplasia of the ipsilateral pedicle. (24)
  • Retrosomatic cleft (pediculolysis, jogger’s fracture). Acquired clefts in one or both pedicles have been reported at all lumbar levels. These are far more common in women (4:1) and may occur in concert with a contralateral pars defect. The increased incidence of these defects in ballet dancers and joggers suggest they may represent ununited stress fractures. (24,26)
  • Retroisthmic cleft (laminolysis). A defect in the lamina has been most commonly reported at the L5 segment and may be seen only on CT. (27) Plain films occasionally show the lesion, referred to as the laminar fragmentation sign. An anteroposterior (AP) recumbent view with the tube angled 45° caudad often reveals the defect to better advantage. (26) The defect can occur as an isolated finding, be associated with an ipsilateral hypoplastic pedicle, or develop as a stress fracture from a contralateral spondylolysis. (25,26,28)

Age of onset supports the argument for the stress fracture origin. Spondylolysis in children can occur shortly after walking begins; however, clinical and radiographic detection occurs most commonly after the age of 5 and most often between the ages of 10 and 15. (7) There appears to be a meaningful causal association among the onset of the pars defect, the development of the lumbar lordosis, and repeated infant falls, particularly if premature walking occurs. (34) Parents should be discouraged from prompting their child to walk before the child is prepared to do so.
An increased prevalence of spondylolysis and spondylolisthesis has been noted in individuals who participate in above-average levels of physical activity, particularly when the activity involves stressful hyperextension movements of the lumbar spine. Rossi (35), in a report to the Italian Olympic Committee in 1978, reviewed 1430 lumbar spine radiographs of 16- to 27-year-old athletes. Of these athletes 239 (16.7%) were found to have spondylolysis and /or spondylolisthesis. The athletic activities in which participants had the highest prevalence were diving and gymnastics. The prevalence among people in those sports was higher than for the general population, supporting the assertion that athletic activity resulting in great spinal mechanical stress is related to the development of spondylolysis and spondylolisthesis. Similarly, a high percentage of pole vaulters develop spondylolysis. (36)
Raynal et al. (37) examined the radiographs of 4619 middleaged manual laborers involved in heavy lifting and found a 9.4% prevalence of spondylolysis and spondylolisthesis, which is similar

to the 10.9% prevalence noted among Japanese athletes. (38) Diving, cricket, fast bowling, gymnastics, weightlifting, and pole vaulting all require repetitive hyperextension of the lumbar spine coupled with jarring, which suggests that hyperextension is a factor in the cause of spondylolysis and spondylolisthesis. Accepting the stress fracture origin presents a challenge. Because all other stress fractures heal with immobilization, why doesn’t this one? The answer appears to be that these individuals are not immobilized at the time the spondylolysis occurs. This results in the non-union of the stress fracture and thus a spondylolysis. Micheli’s group (41,42,43) documented the healing and disappearance of pars defects with the use of the Boston overlap brace (antilordotic brace).
Clinical Features
In addition to their controversial origin, isthmic spondylolysis and spondylolisthesis present a challenging clinical picture. Spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis may be found in symptomatic or asymptomatic individuals. Patients with severe displacement may experience only minimal back pain, and there is no correlation between the degree of anterior displacement and the severity of subjective symptoms. (5,44)
Pain
Of those patients with back pain related to spondylolisthesis, 25% have a recent history of antecedent trauma. (45) These patients have a deep, dull, aching pain that is localized to the area of spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis. In most cases the lesion has been present since the age of 5 or 6 years. (8) Studies indicate that approximately one half of patients with radiologic signs of spondylolisthesis never develop symptoms. (2,34,46,47) Nachemson (48) believes that spondylolysis does not contribute to the cause of pain at all, and according to Schmorl and Junghans (32), pain specifically related to spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis does not exist. In addition, Fredrickson et al. (8), in an extensive prospective study, found that the development of spondylolysis with or without spondylolisthesis does not cause pain in most patients.
The exact pain generator is often elusive, and suggested mechanisms include facet syndromes (especially of L4-L5), sacroiliac joint syndrome, disc tears and/or herniation at L4-L5 or L5-S1, nerve root traction, muscle fatigue syndromes, and periostitis or intervening cleft tissue associated with the defect itself (the so-called spondylolysis ligament). (49) Nerve endings have been demonstrated within the pars defect tissues, which potentially can be a nociceptive site. (49,50) Lumbar pain may often be present in patients developing spondylolysis, but its presence goes unrecognized because of the young age (2-10 years) of the patient and the failure of the parents to acknowledge the child’s complaints, which may be simply disregarded as “growing pains.” The development of spondylolysis in a teenage athlete or young adult is usually painful. (35,36)
The most common neurological abnormality other than back pain is selective nerve root compression either from the enlarged pars pseudo-arthrosis (L5 nerve root), L4-L5 disc herniation (L4, L5, or S1 nerve roots), or disc herniation at L5-S1 (L5 or S1 nerve roots). (51,52) If non-dermatomal leg pain is present, it will likely be a referred sclerotomal pattern from irritation of the mesodermal structures, such as the ligaments and muscles. (53) Central canal stenosis is uncommon because the central canal size actually increases with isthmic spondylolisthesis. Disc herniation at the spondylolisthesis is decidedly uncommon, although internal disc disruption and subsequent degeneration does occur. There is no correlation among the amount of anterolisthesis, pain, or neurological disability. (54)
Physical Examination Findings
Although pain may or may not be associated with the presence of spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis, objective physical findings tend to parallel the degree of slippage, with the exception of neurological disability.
Spondylolysis and Grade 1 Spondylolisthesis.
Detectable physical findings in patients with spondylolysis and slippage up to grade 1 are minimal to absent. Gait is typically unaffected and only subtle postural changes occur. When observable, changes may include hyperlordosis and asymmetrical erector spinae muscle prominence owing to mild rotary lumbar scoliosis that disappears with forward flexion. Palpation of the lumbosacral junction during trunk flexion or straight-leg raising test may reveal a palpable or audible click as a result of movement across the pars (spinal rattle). (55) Associated hamstring tightness is also commonly noted on these maneuvers, although no statistically significant correlation to spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis has been established. An associated spina bifida occulta may be signaled by localized hypertrichosis.
On palpation with the patient prone a step-off defect may be felt as the L5 spinous process stays in normal position and the L4 spinous moves forward. (Fig. 5-14A) With degenerative spondylolisthesis the affected segment’s spinous translates anterior. (Fig. 5-14B) In the side-lying posture the mobility of the posterior arch can be assessed by grasping the spinous process while rotating the spine on the pelvis.
Orthopedic testing is invariably vague in establishing the diagnosis, but the stork test should be included in the examination of any patient with suspected spondylolysis. The stork test, also referred to as the single-leg standing hyperextension test, is performed by having the patient hyperextend the lumbar spine while standing on a single leg. (56,57) (Fig. 5-15) The test loads the posterior elements on the side of stance and is considered positive if the patient’s lower back pain is reproduced. (58) The test should be performed bilaterally. Although normally used for assessment of an active spondylolysis, pain may also indicate irritation of the facet structures.
Grades 2-4 Spondylolisthesis.
With higher degree slips more obvious findings occur. Postural changes can be striking. Prominent hip and knee flexion contractors may be noted secondary to tight hamstring and iliopsoas muscles. An accentuated lumbar lordosis that may extend into the lower thoracic spine is not uncommon (thoracic lordosis). A highly diagnostic gait pattern, referred to as the pelvic waddle or Phalen-Dickson sign, may also result: The stride is short and stiff-legged with associated pelvic rotation on each step. These patients may prefer to jog or run rather than walk. Walking is usually performed on the toes with the knees bent. (59,60) Other observations may include heart-shaped buttocks and bilateral transverse skin furrows above the iliac crest.

Active Versus Inactive
As a result of such inconsistencies in the subjective and objective findings of patients with spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis, we have developed the concept of active versus inactive spondylolysis and /or spondylolisthesis to aid the clinician in deciphering the true cause of the patient’s pain. Unfortunately, the concept of active spondylolysis is still foreign to most clinicians.
Although most pars defects are clinically irrelevant (61), plain film radiography is inadequate to reliably identify those lesions that may be of clinical importance. Radiologic findings are limited to depiction of anatomy and do not provide significant information regarding bone physiology. This morphological approach often does little to identify why a given individual with spondylolysis does or does not have lower back pain. Most patients who experience lower back symptoms related directly to spondylolysis will have a higher metabolic rate (i.e., activity) at the defect. (42,43,62,63) Therefore, the clinical decision-making process may be aided by physiological imaging to determine whether the pars defect(s) is or is not active.
The use of single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT) bone scans and specialized fat-suppression MRI pulse sequences have emerged as the most sensitive methods for detecting clinically active spondylolytic defects (61); however, as reflected by the prevalence of asymptomatic spondylolysis, most pars defects will have metabolically normal bone marrow activity.
Individuals with back pain that is recalcitrant to conservative intervention and has radiographic evidence of spondylolysis should be considered for advanced imaging, particularly if the pain worsens with weight-bearing activity. These individuals are often engaged in athletics that incorporate repetitive-motion activities that load the posterior elements of the lower lumbar vertebral segments.
Questions are often raised about the ability of individuals with spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis to participate in contact sports and heavy lifting in the work environment. By implementing an evaluation process that includes not only physical examination findings and morphological imaging techniques (plain film radiography or CT) but also some form of physiological imaging (such as SPECT bone scan or MRI) one can determine with a high degree of certainty if physical activity needs to be curtailed. (Fig. 5-16) An algorithmic approach to the diagnosis and treatment of patients with spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis is available. This approach begins with clinical assessment and plain film radiographs and ends with a select few patients receiving physiological imaging (SPECT bone scan or MRI) to determine whether the spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis is clinically active. (Figs. 5-17 and 5-18)

Imaging Features
Imaging is pivotal in the diagnosis and management of spondylolysis and spondylolisthesis. Plain films, CT, MRI, and nuclear bone scan are the modalities used. (29)
Plain Film Radiographic Examination
A five-view series is optimum for diagnosis and characterization of these defects; the views are AP, lateral, bilateral anterior obliques, and an AP angulated lumbosacral spot view. Weight-bearing AP views are essential for demonstrating the commonly associated lumbar scoliosis (olisthetic scoliosis). Weight-bearing lateral views are necessary for maximizing the anterolisthesis, which may not be present on a supine study. (54) Oblique studies are often omitted but will show a larger proportion of symptomatic separations in patients < 30 years of age. (67) These views demonstrate up to 10-20% more pars defects than the AP and lateral views alone but still miss up to 13% of defects. (27,67) Flexion-extension studies can be performed for the assessment of instability and are best performed with the patient in an erect weight-bearing position.
AP Non-Angulated View.
AP views of the lumbar spine need to be closely scrutinized for signs of spondylolysis and spondylolisthesis, which can be subtle. If the radiograph is taken lying down, many cases that would appear to be spondylolysis alone (no slip) will show slippage on erect spinal films. CT is the most definitive imaging modality for demonstrating the pars defects.
  • Pars defect. The normal pars interarticularis is not easily discernible on the non-angulated AP view because of projectional distortion produced by the presence of the lordotic lumbosacral angle. The pars interarticularis on this view projects immediately inferior and slightly medial to the pedicle outline. A pars defect will sometimes be visible as an irregular or smooth linear radiolucency approximately paralleling the inferior cortex of the adjacent pedicle.
  • Inverted Napoleon’s hat (gendarme’s cap). In grade 2 or greater spondylolisthesis the inferior L5 vertebral body and transverse processes will become superimposed over the sacral base and ala. The subsequent superimposition of these lumbosacral structures creates a characteristic hat-like appearance. (68) (Fig. 5-5)
  • Bowline of Brailsford. This term is used when the inferior border of the inverted Napoleon’s hat corresponds to the anterior aspect of the L5 vertebral body with the attached transverse processes. (68) (Fig. 5-5)
  • Spinous process deviation (tilt) sign. Malalignment of a lumbar spinous can be a sign of a posterior arch deficiency, such as spondylolysis, facet agenesis, and pedicle agenesis or hypoplasia. (69,70) The spinous process of the involved segment will reveal this tilt sign in up to 75% of cases. (70) The deviation may be more obvious on upright studies.
  • Spina bifida occulta. In the presence of L5 spina bifida occulta there is a 13 times greater incidence of L5 spondylolysis. (21,71) An S1 spina bifida occulta is estimated to be present in up to 50% of L5 spondylolistheses. (72)
  • Sclerotic pedicle sign (Wilkinson’s syndrome). Sclerosis and enlargement are usually associated with unilateral defects but can be seen in bilateral cases, particularly when healing is occurring.
  • Olisthetic scoliosis. Scoliosis in association with spondylolisthesis is found in almost 25% of cases. The cause is often difficult to determine, but 30% of cases may be the result of greater slippage on one side, possibly related to the asynchronous appearance of the defect on each side (olisthetic scoliosis). (73) More commonly, the cause is muscle spasm owing to pain. (74) This has been reported in at least 40% of cases. (74) Scoliosis in asymptomatic spondylolisthesis occurs in 6% of cases. (74)

For the AP angulated view the central ray is directed about midway between the symphysis pubis and the umbilicus (approximately at the level of the anterosuperior iliac spine) with 20° of cephalic angulation to pass through the lumbosacral disc space. This removes the effect of the lumbar lordosis, and the pars are displayed to advantage (75,76); the spondylolysis appear as lucent clefts beneath the inferior pedicle outline. Instability Evaluation
An unstable spondylolisthesis is defined as having excessive mobility of the spondylolisthetic segment. The clinical implications
P.454

usually include failure to respond to conservative treatment and the need for consideration of surgical options. The radiological criteria for unstable spondylolisthesis is controversial, but typically includes documentation of progressive anterolisthesis, high-grade slip (> 25%), sacral doming with trapezoidal L5 vertebral body, and larger slip angles and excessive mobility on dynamic stress radiography. (48) Imaging studies should be performed with weight bearing whenever possible; comparison studies should be done in the same way to maximize reliability. Comparison of recumbent and erect studies is fundamentally flawed and inappropriate. When the risk for progression or increasing instability is assessed as high, the radiographs in flexion-extension may be indicated at 6- to 12-month intervals, especially during adolescent growth periods.
  • Progressive anterolisthesis. The peak time for increased slip has a bimodal distribution, with one peak at the prepubertal growth spurt and the other at the onset of disc degeneration, which can be as early as 25 years of age. Small grade 1 slips generally do not progress more than 1% after they occur. (8)
  • High-grade slip. Children who have a ≥50% spondylolisthesis have increased frequency of listhesis progression and excessive mobility. (82,84)
  • Sacral doming and vertebral body wedging. With slips > 30% the altered compressive forces through the lumbosacral junction produce acquired growth deformities of L5 (posterior wedging, trapezoidal) and S1 (rounded sacral promontory, doming). (10) These features are typical for an adolescent-acquired pars defect with predisposition to higher grade displacement, progression, and excessive motion in both angular rotation and sagittal plane translation. (85) The same changes occur in type I dysplastic spondylolisthesis, which is also associated with progressive and larger-magnitude slips.
  • Large slip angles. Though controversial, some authors place emphasis on the axial axis rotation of L5 over the sacrum as a key marker of instability. (71,84) Measurements > 40-50° are considered unstable.

Pre-Employment Radiographic Screening
In many countries, including the United States, pre-employment low-back radiographs are used to screen employees for potential risk of injury in the workplace. Often, patients are denied employment or promotion because of the radiographic visualization of an asymptomatic (inactive) lumbar spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis. (93,94) Based on the performance of the athletes in the previously cited studies, this attitude may not be justified. (32,38, 64,65) We found no studies demonstrating that workers with spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis involved in the heavy labor sector of industry are at any greater risk for back injuries or have any greater loss of work time than those employees without spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis. A plethora of personal opinions exist to the contrary; these opinions, however, cannot be supported by documented clinical case studies or research data. It is our belief that refusal of employment based on the presence of spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis is as antiquated as the procedure of pre-employment radiographic screening itself, which is now receiving considerably less emphasis. (93,94)
Until the true cause of low back pain in patients with these lesions is definitively proved, there should be no discrimination against workers with coincidental asymptomatic (inactive) spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis.

SPECT
Radionuclide skeletal scintigraphy plays a major role in the identification and evaluation of early lesions by providing correlative activity in radiographically occult defects. Because of its reduced resolution, routine planar imaging—planar bone scintigraphy (PBS)—frequently does not depict abnormal focal uptake sites and may fail to reveal spondylolysis in as many as 53% of cases. (95) SPECT combines the sensitivity of radionuclide tracer uptake and the specificity of cross-sectional and multiplanar imaging and is ideally suited to demonstrating subtle bone lesions, especially in the spine. (Fig. 5-28) SPECT imaging is also particularly useful in the diagnosis of subtle pathologies and early phases of a wide spectrum of bone disease, such as infection, tumor, arthritis, fractures, and spondylolysis. When the diagnosis for back pain remains elusive, especially in athletes, SPECT imaging is pivotal for early diagnosis, follow-up imaging choices, and providing information on therapy response. (Fig. 5-29)
Technique.
In the delayed phase (2-3 hr after injection), routine planar images are obtained of the skeleton. SPECT images are then taken with a circular tomographic gantry device, similar to a CT scanner. The gantry houses three or more detector heads that rotate around the patient in a step-like motion. The resultant images are displayed as multiple cross sections that provide a third (axial) dimension. Reconstructed sagittal and coronal images are routinely obtained from the axial data sets. The improved detail of the spine allows one to locate lesions to a specific segment and anatomic component.

Evaluation of Spondylolysis and Spondylolisthesis.
Generally, bone scans show increased uptake at an active pars defect that may be either recent in onset or attempting to heal. Defects that are old have no mobility or are healed and usually show no abnormality.
SPECT imaging improves diagnostic sensitivity over planar images for an active defect by up to 50% and is the most reliable imaging correlative sign as a cause for pain, especially acute pain. (42,43,62,63) (Fig. 5-30) Correlative imaging, especially MRI, should be performed to exclude other causes for the increased activity, such as facet osteoarthritis, osteoid osteoma, osteoblastoma, or metastatic disease.
SPECT studies in defects of the pars interarticularis can be crucial and frequently drive patient management. For athletes, especially those at high risk (gymnasts, weightlifters, pole vaulters, football players, pitchers, and cricketers), the onset of back pain may be clarified by demonstrating an active pars separation with a positive SPECT study. A positive scan often occurs before a cleft can be demonstrated on CT or MRI ( preradiographic phase or pending) and may allow treatment that prevents separation and non-union. (96,97) Such patients reduce their physical activity to reverse the stress reaction within the pars and cease or retard the development of pars defects.
When SPECT findings confirm clinical suspicion of pain secondary to chronic stress-related pars reaction, with or without actual spondylolysis, treatment with an antilordotic orthosis (e.g., Boston overlap brace) should be instituted. (41) Findings from SPECT imaging can also be useful for indicating when a young athlete can return to competition. After an appropriate time of bracing the SPECT scan is repeated; once findings are normal, activity can be gradually resumed to competitive levels.
Trauma with Pre-Existing Spondylolisthesis: A Problematic Circumstance.
A distinct challenge to the clinician occurs when a patient presents with lumbosacral pain after a traumatic event. Often there is no history of back pain before the recent injury. Radiographic examination reveals an L4 or L5 isthmic type IIA spondylolisthesis (bilateral pars defects), which is smooth and well corticated. The clinical dilemma is to determine whether the current trauma is related to the presence of spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis or is unrelated and whether the pars defect is generating symptoms that are contributing to the lumbosacral pain. The presence of bilateral, smooth, well-corticated pars defects usually indicates a pre-existing chronic lesion. Acute fractures of the pars are seldom bilateral and symmetrical.
The next question that must be addressed is whether the recent trauma has resulted in significant exacerbation of the pre-existing spondylolysis through local injury and/or increased displacement. This is a valid question that requires further diagnostic imaging. It is recommended that flexion-extension or traction-compression lateral lumbar stress radiographs be performed to determine if segmental instability is present. (See “Instability Evaluation.”) The presence of instability suggests that the recent traumatic episode may be implicated as a causal factor in the production of the patient’s current lower back pain. Additional special imaging to include SPECT will prove helpful in searching for increased physiologic activity of the subchondral bone at the level of the pars defect. A positive SPECT scan helps substantiate a direct causeand-effect relationship between the trauma and the patient’s current lumbosacral pain. A negative SPECT scan, coupled with negative stress radiographs, forces the clinician to look elsewhere, past the spondylolisthesis, for a biomechanical or organic cause for the patient’s lumbosacral pain.

The Surgical Spine.
SPECT imaging has a role to play in spinal surgery, primarily for postoperative assessments for complications.
  • Postsurgical evaluation. The major applications are in the diagnosis of pseudo-arthrosis within a fusion mass, hardware loosening, complicating arthritis, and infection.
  • Pseudo-arthrosis. Non-union within a fusion mass can be painful and difficult to localize with anatomic imaging. Focal uptake on bone scan is diagnostic, and SPECT doubles the diagnostic sensitivity. (98)
  • Hardware loosening. Fixation constructs are commonly implanted but may undergo loosening at the bone-metal interface. SPECT imaging can show increased bone activity at these sites but requires multiplanar analysis because the hardware implants can overlap the interfaces, preventing them being imaged.
  • Facet joint instability and spondylolysis acquisita. Increased uptake at joints distant to the fusion can be documented with SPECT and may relate to the onset of progressive back pain. (99)
  • Infection. Concentration of isotope at opposing endplates from infection across a disc space is best shown on sagittal SPECT images.
  • Presurgical evaluation or pathology screening. Planar scan with selective SPECT at the site of interest may assist in defining unsuspected pathologies, including tumor and Paget’s disease, which not only may alter the surgical approach but also may prevent surgery.
  • Spondylolisthetic instability. Patients with an active defect as shown by SPECT imaging and who are unresponsive to conservative management may benefit from fusion surgery. (100)
CT
CT has traditionally been the method of choice for demonstrating the bony detail of the pars defect and is more accurate than plain film radiographs and MRI. Up to almost 10% of defects shown on CT will not be visible on plain film. (101) Detection of tandem pathology such as disc herniation at L4-L5 and nerve compression can also be made, though these are better depicted by MRI. The routine angled gantry scans parallel to the L5-S1 disc space frequently do not show the pars defect because the beam passes parallel through the gap. Reversed angled gantry studies are preferred because they are obtained perpendicular to the plane of the defect and show the bone separation with greater sensitivity and specificity. Both axial soft tissue and bone windows are viewed with sagittal reconstructions. Multislice helical CT offers the advantage of thin section images and three-dimensional renditions of the lesion.
Pars Defect.
Axial CT images allow definitive diagnosis of the fracture zone. (Fig. 5-23D) The anatomic localization of the cleft to the pars can be made, excluding other known locations, such as the neurocentral synchondrosis, pedicle (pediculolysis), or lamina (retroisthmic). An asymmetrical appearance is common. A spectrum of changes at the pars can be found, including sclerosis, irregularity, corticated smooth surfaces, and bulbous enlargement. Cartilage, fluid, or fat may be interposed in the cleft, and complete or incomplete bony bridging may also be present. Differentiating the defect from a normal facet joint can sometimes be difficult but is usually accomplished on sagittal or three-dimensional reconstructions. On axial images the facet joints lie at the disc level and are smooth, are concave-convex, and often have a small notch posteriorly on the medial cortical surface at the insertion of the joint capsule. The demonstration of at least one image that shows a complete neural arch ring (incomplete ring sign) excludes a defect. (102) Rotation between L5 and the sacrum can also be seen, confirming the cause of any accompanying scoliosis (olisthetic scoliosis).
Neural Arch Changes.
Other signs of bony stress can be seen in the pedicle and lamina, including cortical and trabecular thickening and enlargement. The asymmetric growth of the lamina can often be observed and is associated with spinous deviation or spina bifida. The spinal canal is elongated sagittally. (78)
Nerve Effects.
Compression of individual nerves can be observed, although central canal stenosis is uncommon. The L5 nerve root is the most prone to compression. Compression can be a result of an enlarged fragmented defect or lateral recess encroachment from L4-L5 disc herniation (far out disc syndrome); it can also occur secondarily to nerve root stretching with loss of disc height and traction by the L5 pedicle or sacral ala.
Discal Pathology.
Axial CT slices often show apparent posterior bulging of the L5 disc at the level of spondylolisthesis (pseudo-disc herniation), which occurs when limited gantry angulation cannot exactly match the angle of slice selection to the angle of the disc and endplate. (8) Discographically proven disc damage with annular tears or herniation above the L5 spondylolisthesis occurs in up to 50% of cases. (52) Retrolisthesis of L4 on L5 by 2-8 mm is observed in > 40% of L5 spondylolistheses, of which half will have a discal abnormality. (52)
MRI
Occasionally, patients with suspected spondylolysis may present to clinicians after having obtained normal plain films, normal planar bone scans, and MRI studies that are interpreted as normal for disc herniation or stenosis. This can occur if the radiologist did not specifically look for bone marrow edema on the parasagittal or axial images.
One of us (TRY) has had experience with such a case; the MRI scans were read as normal when, in fact, retrospective analysis of the left and right parasagittal T2-weighted images and axial T1-weighted images revealed bone marrow edema in the area of the L4 pars in a highly active 16-year-old boy who was performing repetitive hyperextension activities as a football player. The plainfilm radiographs and planar bone scan were normal, and it was not until a SPECT scan was performed that the true location of the back pain was revealed. (Fig. 5-31) On the basis of this case, we suggest that all low back MRI studies, particularly in active athletes, include parasagittal T2-weighted images and axial T1- and T2-weighted images through the area of the pedicle-pars junction to rule out the possibility of bone marrow edema in patients with active spondylolysis. Overall, the recognition of isthmic spondylolysis with MRI of the lumbar spine has received little attention in the literature. (103,104,105)

Pars Defect.
Sagittal T1-weighted and proton density studies are the best sequences for showing a pars separation because they give better anatomic definition and provide a high marrow signal on which the low-intensity defect can be appreciated. (106) Sclerosis appears as low signal on all sequences and can hide a true separation, unless there is high signal on T1-weighted (fat) or T2-weighted (fluid) studies within the cleft. Bone fragments are not usually well shown, although the fibrocartilaginous mass will be of intermediate signal on all sequences and may be seen impinging on the adjacent exiting L5 nerve root. These marrow changes, although highly sensitive, lack specificity. False positives include tumor infiltration, sclerotic pars, volume averaging of adjacent facet, and surgical facetectomy. (106)
A frequently overlooked subtle sign of a pars fracture in an early phase, before the emergence of a defect, is bone marrow edema. This is best demonstrated on T2-weighted and short tau inversion recovery (STIR) sequences and will appear as increased signal. This finding often correlates with a site of increased uptake seen on SPECT nuclear imaging. In young patients with back pain, especially those involved in athletic activity, the inclusion of a sagittal or axial-fat suppressed sequences should be considered to demonstrate a prespondylolisthetic pars lesion or a “pending” pars defect. (Fig. 5-31) The bone marrow edema may persist for months, even when rest and bracing are instituted, similar to stress fractures in the extremities; it closely correlates with the presence of back pain. Its resolution can be a marker for completed healing.
Nerve Effects.
The involved foramen takes on an altered shape, its long axis becoming more horizontal. The relationship of the exiting L5 nerve root to the defect, fibrocartilaginous mass at the pars, discovertebral margins, and facet joint is best assessed on sagittal images. The shape of the foramen, the contained nerve and its course, the amount of surrounding epidural fat, and any alteration in the course of the nerve should be noted. (107) A figure of eight foraminal shape (hourglass) is caused by narrowing in the waist of the foramen and is a marker of anterolisthesis. Bulging of the posterior disc and downward displacement of the inferior margin of the pars defect may be seen. A fibrocartilaginous mass may be present adjacent to the defect. These can all combine to produce exiting L5 nerve root compression.
In 25% of spondylolisthesis cases the L5 nerve is normal, 25% have a nerve that cannot be differentiated from the adjacent disc-pars tissue, and 50% have signs of compression with deformity and altered course. (108) The spinal canal at the level of the slip is increased in sagittal diameter (wide canal sign), which is a reliable marker for an isthmic defect as the cause. (78) Instead of anterior slippage of the vertebral bodies, the posterior elements are occasionally subluxed posteriorly. (109)

Discal Pathology.
The disc space at the involved level may take a number of appearances. Normal hydration and preserved height are common in small slips with the posterior discal margin oblique in orientation. With progressive degeneration there is loss of T2-weighted high signal and decreased height; the posterior annulus may bulge upwardly, with its convex surface forming the floor of the exit foramen. Posterior herniation is a rare finding. Tandem disc pathology at L4-L5 may be seen with retrolisthesis, dehydration, loss of height, circumferential disc bulging, and frank herniation.
Treatment and Prognosis
Conservative Management
Considerable controversy exists concerning the treatment and management of patients with spondylolisthesis. The conservative medical approach to treatment includes the following: restriction of physical activity, strengthening exercises for abdominal and back muscles, bedrest, low back bracing, and non-narcotic analgesics. (65)
Chiropractic manipulation has also proven to be a beneficial conservative approach to treatment of back pain in patients with spondylolisthesis by addressing the associated biomechanical alterations. (53) A large majority of patients with inactive spondylolisthesis have a related hyperlordosis, facet syndrome, and/or sacroiliac fixation, and these conditions are often the cause of symptoms, not the spondylolisthesis. (53,110)
According to Illi (111), restoration of normal sacroiliac joint mobility may decrease the strain on the lower lumbar spine, thereby giving patients relief from lower back pain (which he thought was an indirect effect of the lumbar spondylolisthesis). Avoiding direct manipulation of the segment with pars defects, Cassidy et al. (53) reported an 80% favorable response to manipulation of either the sacroiliac joint or the lumbar zygapophyseal joints above the involved segment. Prone recoil manipulation directly over the area of spondylolisthesis is contraindicated. (112) Spinal manipulation offers rapid symptomatic relief to many patients with back pain who coincidentally have spondylolisthesis.
Taylor (113) found that the motion segment above spondylolisthesis, usually L4-L5, is the segment of highest biomechanical load and thus often the site of joint dysfunction and /or pain. Comparing 60 patients with spondylolisthesis to 60 patients without, Taylor found significantly higher intersegmental hyperextension at the segment above the spondylolytic defect than in those without spondylolisthesis. This intersegmental hyperextension has been associated with mechanical facet pain by other researchers. (114,115)
The successful resolution of back pain in spondylolisthesis often occurs only if the level above, usually L4-L5, is evaluated in regard to biomechanical load. (116) Clinical examination should delineate the nature of the biomechanical lesion involved and dictate the appropriate manipulative treatment. (116) Occasionally it is stated that manipulation may be contraindicated in spondylolisthesis. This opinion, however, is unfounded, and Mierau et al. (112) suggest that those with spondylolisthesis may in fact respond to manipulation more quickly than those without. Comparing 25 patients with spondylolisthesis and low back pain with 260 patients with low back pain alone, Mierau et al. (112) obtained good results with manipulation in 80% of patients with spondylolisthesis versus only 70% of those without. The number of treatments required for a good result were 6.9 and 7.5, respectively, for the two groups.
The question often arises as to the relative safety of participation in contact sports for a patient with spondylolisthesis. (116) The often-used hypothesis has been that the segment is unstable; therefore, the individual is at an increased risk of cord or nerve root damage. (116) This is probably true only in a patient with grade 3 or 4 displacement and significant disc degeneration. Patients with lower grades are probably quite stable (87), and these individuals may even be afforded some protection from the usual low back neurogenic syndromes because of the increase in sagittal dimensions of the spinal canal at the spondylolisthetic segment.
Boston Overlap Brace in Acute Spondylolysis.
Sportsrelated injuries to the spine are seen with increasing frequency in such varied sports as gridiron football, gymnastics, weightlifting, diving, and pole vaulting. Because school screening programs for scoliosis and other structural deformities of the spine have improved the early detection of deformities in young adolescents, a spinal brace that would maintain adequate immobilization and positioning and yet allow continued participation in normal adolescent activities has become necessary. (41) Although use of exercises in conjunction with the traditional Milwaukee brace system has been repeatedly emphasized, that brace, with its metal superstructure and leather outriggers, makes vigorous athletic participation difficult, particularly in contact sports.
The Boston brace system is a modification of the Milwaukee brace. The initial Boston module consisted of an outer shell of polypropylene with an inner lining and padding system of foam polyethylene that could be adapted to most body builds. One of the basic objectives in orthopedic management of low back pain of varied causes is attainment of an antilordotic posture. (41) Traditional Williams flexion exercises are designed to decrease the lordosis of the low back and increase flexibility. A number of braces and pelvic supports have been designed to aid in this management approach by compressing the abdominal contents, thereby decreasing the mechanical load on the spine. (41) However, none is truly effective in decreasing the lumbar lordosis. When antilordotic capabilities of the pelvic mechanisms were fully understood, use of the brace system in the management of certain back pain problems in adolescent patients seemed appropriate. (41) Immobilization of the spine could be maintained with full activities, including athletic participation, with the appropriate bracing procedure.
Use of a new, semirigid lumbosacral orthosis that directly decreases the lumbar lordosis and lessens axial loading of the spine by increasing intra-abdominal pressure to limit the pelvic torsion of the trunk seems promising on both theoretical and clinical grounds. (41) (Fig. 5-32) When used full time for 3-6 months in conjunction with a flexibility and muscle-strengthening exercise program, the Boston brace has been shown to be successful in managing the pain in > 80% of young patients. (41) According to Steiner and Micheli (117) use of the Boston overlap antilordotic brace in the treatment of 67 patients with symptomatic spondylolysis and spondylolisthesis yielded excellent or good results with no pain and return to full activities in 52 (78%) of these cases.

Spondylolisthesis and Foot Pronation.
It is imperative that the complete clinical assessment of the patient with lower back pain and pre-existing stable spondylolytic spondylolisthesis include an evaluation of the lower extremity. One of the most common lower extremity disorders implicated in chronic structural biomechanical dysfunction is excessive hyperpronation of the longitudinal arch. This medial shift and internal rotation of the midfoot reduces the foot’s ability to properly balance body weight and cushion the force of heel-strike shock. (81) This stressful weightbearing imbalance can lead to bone marrow edema and inflammatory conditions of the lower extremity. (118,119)
Patients with spondylolisthesis often have a swayback postural fault with an anterior pelvic tilt. Pronation of the longitudinal arch accentuates this. (120) Excessive pronation can progress as a serial distortion, creating a functional leg length inequality with presenting symptoms of sciatic pain, unilateral hip symptoms, and lower back pain. (121) Leg length inequality puts stress on the pelvic structures, causing an inward hip rotation and /or torquing of the pelvis, often resulting in chronic myofascial back pain, which is common among people with spondylolisthesis. (30)
Research on spondylolisthesis patients has revealed a greater reduction in pain scores when orthotic therapy was combined with spinal manipulation. (17,45) Flexible orthotics enhance the biomechanics of the lower extremity by modifying minor deficits that inhibit the integrity of the pedal foundation. (18) Flexible orthotics have also been shown to decrease heel-strike shock, significantly reduce pronation, and reduce pronation velocity. (7,11,15,19) Custom-made flexible orthotics have demonstrated improvement of foot-ankle alignment, knee alignment, and pelvic leveling. (4,5,23) These measures often have a positive effect on reducing leg length inequality. (6) Orthotics have a positive effect on dynamic function in terms of balance (eyes open), proprioceptive balance (eyes closed), pelvic movement patterns during gait, and athletic performance. (8,9,10)
To prescribe the most effective support, a weight-bearing casting method to obtain quantifiable information on the extent of pedal imbalance is recommended. (12,13) Casting the feet in a position of function gives real-life information on which corrections can be based. (14) This method also results in a more accurate fit, since the true length and width of the foot during the closed-chain stance are thoroughly assessed.
It is our opinion that orthotic therapy coupled with spinal manipulation represents a cost-effective, preventative, and integrated approach to case management of the inactive spondylolisthetic patient.
SPECT Classification of Problematic Patients.
Four distinct groups of patients with spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis can be identified based on clinical examination and imaging, including scintigraphy. (122) Classification may be helpful in the overall management of these patients.
  • Patients with unilateral or bilateral focal scintigraphic abnormalities without radiographic evidence of spondylolysis.

    These patients are in the preradiographic “pending” phase of spondylolysis, and early immobilization may halt the development of an active spondylolytic defect. In view of the high sensitivity but low specificity of scintigraphy, caution must be exercised before rendering a definitive diagnosis of acute or active spondylolysis because other abnormalities, such as osteoid osteoma or other benign bone tumors of the neural arch, could create such a focal uptake.
  • Patients with radiographic evidence of spondylolysis and increased uptake on the scintigram. Healing is still progressing in these patients, and there is a possibility of bony union with immobilization using an antilordotic Boston overlap brace.   • Patients with unilateral spondylolysis and enhanced radiotracer uptake on the contralateral side (Wilkinson’s syndrome). These patients are at imminent risk of developing bilateral spondylolysis. Appropriate immobilization in an antilordotic brace (Boston overlap) should be considered.
  • Patients with radiographically confirmed spondylolysis without focal accumulation on scintigraphy. In these patients, there is an established non-union of the previous stress fracture, which is unlikely to heal with immobilization (inactive spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis). This may have medicolegal implications for patients examined within 1 year of an accident alleged to be the cause of a spondylolysis.

Complications
Non-Union.
The pathophysiology for non-union most likely is delayed diagnosis, failure to rest adequately, inherent biomechanics placing stress at the pars, and a poor blood supply (which is derived from the periosteum and is continually being damaged). Short-term studies have shown that non-union of pars defects does not hinder the return to athletics. (123)
Progressive Displacement.
There has been debate in the literature concerning the progression of anterior displacement in spondylolisthesis. Only 2-3% of adult patients with spondylolisthesis show progressive displacement. (8) The majority of these cases occur as a result of underlying degenerative changes, usually disc collapse. (32) The presence of an anterior osteophyte (sacral buttress) usually denotes stability with little chance for progressive slip. (17)
Progression of the anterior displacement in spondylolisthesis occurs primarily between the ages of 5 and 10 years, although some cases may continue to displace in patients up to 18 years of age. (8) Maximum anterior displacement is usually seen within 18-24 months during and after the development of spondylolysis, which is usually < 10 years of age. (8) Very few cases demonstrate further anterolisthesis beyond the age of 18 years. (2,8) (Fig. 5-33) Cessation of athletic activity does not influence the
P.466

degree of slippage and is contemplated only in the presence of pain correlated with a positive bone scan (active).
Known risk factors for childhood progression include adolescent growth spurt, female sex, pain, spina bifida occulta and other associated posterior arch dysplastic (type I) changes, doming of the sacral promontory, trapezoidal-shaped L5 vertebral body, grade 2 or greater lesion, marked sagittal rotation of L5 on the sacrum (slip angle), and high mobility on flexion-extension studies. (17,124,125) The sacral surface doming and posterior wedging of the L5 vertebral body often occur in tandem and are acquired findings as a result of the anterolisthesis impairing growth. (8) Pregnancy does not appear to precipitate any increased slippage or be a factor for obstructed labor, unless there is a breech presentation. Even significant trauma to the spine with a pre-existing spondylolisthesis usually does not increase the slip. (32)
Cases of pain-producing slips are cited in the historical literature (2,34) but are less common in the contemporary citations. There is no correlation between the amount of anterolisthesis and pain. (54) Evolving pars defects, however, especially in young athletes who have positive bone scans or bone marrow edema on MRI, are associated with a high incidence of pain. (42,43,126)
Surgical Management
Therapeutic Injections.
Image-guided injection of a long-acting anesthetic agent and corticosteroid into the pars region or adjacent facet joints may be applied empirically in conservative treatment-resistant cases. Such injections not only are therapeutic but also can be diagnostic in isolating a pain-producing site.
Surgical Indicators.
Four clinical scenarios indicate possible surgical intervention: (a) unstable or progressive spondylolisthesis; (b) nerve compression syndromes; (c) failed conservative management; and (d ) age < 10 years with a grade 2 or greater slip, features of a dysplastic spondylolisthesis, and progressive slippage with neurological deficit.
Surgical Methods.
A spectrum of surgical methods have been employed and a full description is beyond the scope of this chapter. (1,8,11,81,106,125,127,128,129,130) Essentially three categories of surgery are performed: fusion, decompression, and a combination of both. Essentially, fusion is performed when instability or progressive slip has been documented and decompression is used when nerve root involvement is determined. Asymptomatic spondylolistheses usually do not require surgery. (127)
Spinal Fusion.
The most common fusion historically employed for grade 1-2 slips is a single-level, bilateral posterolateral fusion with bone graft of the transverse processes to the sacral ala. (5,131) These are performed as in situ procedures in which there is no attempt to reduce the anterolisthesis. The relatively high non-union rate (10 -15%) for these isolated posterior fusions has led to the development of more contemporary procedures that use various hardware constructs, such as pedicle screws with rods and interbody cage placement. The decision to extend the fusion to L4-L5 is largely based on concordant pain and an abnormal disc demonstrated on MRI or discography. (52) In symptomatic spondylolysis in patients < 30 years of age, bilateral cerclage wire stabilization to connect the transverse processes with the spinous process (Scott wiring technique) has been advocated as a relatively minimally invasive technique. (106) A variant of the technique that employs more robust pedicle screws with laminar hooks (Morscher’s technique) has been also used.
Decompression.
Complete removal of the posterior arch, including the fibrocartilaginous mass of the defect, was historically performed (Gill’s procedure) but is not done in isolation because of the inherent instability it produces. The procedure is frequently complicated by progressive slip. Instead, more selective decompressions are preferred, depending on the anatomic site of nerve compression (laminectomy, foraminotomy, pedicular resection).
Fusion with Decompression.
In the presence of instability and nerve root compression a combined procedure is performed. This is especially the case when a wide-based decompression is performed that will precipitate instability. Operative techniques for higher grades of spondylolisthesis ( grade 3 or higher) are usually combined procedures with in situ positions. (128,129,130) The risk for severe neurological complications, including L5 nerve root injury and cauda equina syndrome, is significantly higher, especially if reduction of the slip is attempted. (129)
Complications.
The usual postsurgical complications apply to spondylolisthesis, for example, non-union of the fusion, hardware failure (fracture, loosening), infection, epidural fibrosis, dural tear, nerve injury, and cauda equina syndrome. The treatment of advanced spondylolisthesis (grades 3- 4) is controversial. The surgical options include decompressive laminectomy, fusion in situ, decompression followed by spinal fusion, and reduction followed by spinal fusion. (128,129) Reduction of the deformity before fusion carries the risk of neurologic deterioration, whereas fusion in situ is thought to be a more reliable option. Decompressive laminectomy is also said to be a safe procedure.
Maurice and Morley (129) reported four cases in which cauda equina lesions developed after posterior spinal fusion for severe advanced spondylolisthesis—three after intertransverse fusion in situ and one after decompression laminectomy. These patients were 16, 15, 15, and 10 years old. All had in situ fusion and subsequently developed cauda equina signs, including bladder dysfunction, lax anal tone, and altered perineal sensations. Partial recovery was noted in two of the four patients. These findings highlight the possible dangers of surgery in patients with spondylolisthesis.
The risk of neurologic damage is well recognized if reduction is attempted before fusion, but it should be noted that fusion in situ and decompressive laminectomy may be complicated by neurologic complications, even in the hands of experienced spinal surgeons. (129) It is important that all patients be counseled before surgery about the risks of neurologic damage from surgical fusion for spondylolisthesis.
Bone graft harvesting from the iliac wing may be complicated with persistent pain, infection, and fracture that may interfere with the sacroiliac joint integrity. After fusion the next mobile disc level will undergo premature degeneration (transitional syndrome) and is the major reason extension of fusion to the L4-L5 level at the time of the initial surgery is often performed. Fusion, involving L5-S1, in a rare case, has shown to increase loads onto the sacrum, resulting in a sacral stress fracture. (132) Spondylolisthesis acquisita, the development of a new pars defect above the level of fusion, has also been documented, although it is uncommon. (81,133)

The American Medical Association (AMA) (86) recommends the following classification for spondylolisthesis in patients who have not been operated on for spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis.
  • Spondylolysis or grade 1-2 spondylolisthesis (up to 50%) that is accompanied by a medically documented but stable injury and medically documented pain and rigidity, with or without muscle spasm: 7% impairment of the whole person.
  • Grade 3-4 spondylolisthesis (up to 100% anterior displacement) that is accompanied by a medically documented but stable injury and medically documented pain and rigidity, with or without muscle spasm: 9% impairment of the whole person.

Imaging Features
The imaging diagnosis of unilateral isthmic spondylolysis is made less commonly if only routine frontal and lateral radiographs are obtained. It requires studies of high technical detail and close attention to detail on interpretations. Oblique studies are essential for revealing these subtle defects; at least 20% more defects are visible only on this view. (5) CT is the most accurate method for showing the separation, although SPECT nuclear imaging is probably the most sensitive modality for the initial detection and provides information on the condition’s role in the origin of back pain.
  • Anterolisthesis. Most cases never show anterolisthesis, which explains why such lesions are detected only on an oblique study. (6) If slip is present, it is usually < 5-10% (grade 1). (Fig. 5-39) In such cases CT may show a contralateral healed defect.
  • Sclerotic pedicle. Many cases are first diagnosed on the basis of this finding and have been referred to as Wilkinson’s syndrome. (7) Sclerosis typically occurs within the contralateral pedicle but can sometimes be on the same side.
  • Contralateral pedicle sclerosis. At least 20% of the weight-bearing forces are distributed through the posterior arch and transferred to and from the vertebral body via the pedicles. A defect in one pars forces these loading stresses to be transferred through the contralateral pedicle, which, by Wolff’s law, induces trabecular and cortical thickening, sclerosis with a diminished marrow space, and, occasionally, enlargement. These findings are readily seen on frontal radiographs, AP tilt-up views of the lumbosacral junction, and oblique views. (Fig. 5-40) Regression of the compensatory pedicle sclerosis can occur after the development of a bilateral defect. (8,9) (Fig. 5-41)
  • Ipsilateral pedicle sclerosis. Infrequently exuberant healing of the defect produces localized sclerosis, which can extend into the pedicle.
  • Spinous process tilt sign. A significantly misaligned spinous process (deviated spinous process sign, spinous tilt sign), as seen on the frontal radiograph and CT, is a potential sign of a unilateral pars defect, agenesis of the pedicle, or agenesis of the facet joint. (9) The deviant spinous rotates to the side of the defect in 75% of cases.
  • Spina bifida occulta. More than 20% of L5 unilateral spondylolyses will have an associated spina bifida occulta as a tandem lesion. (9) The combination of the spinous and pars clefts isolates the lamina to become a freefloating, unattached bony hemilaminar segment, which may be more commonly associated with underlying nerve compression. (10)
  • Lamina sclerosis and fragmentation. The cortex is thicker and sclerotic and has a reduced marrow space. Bony fragmentation on the laminar side of the defect can be seen in 5% of cases (laminar fragmentation sign). (11) Occasionally, a contralateral retroisthmic defect is present. (12)
  • Facet dysplasia. Frequently, the ipsilateral facet joint is deformed.

CLINICAL CONCLUSIONS
Although the true origin of spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis has been unclear for many years, stress fracture has emerged as the most likely cause for the isthmic type IIA. Some populations demonstrate an apparent genetic predisposition to spondylolysis. There is a poorly defined relationship between spondylolisthesis and the origin of back pain. The potential sites for pain include the fibrocartilaginous mass at the defect, disc, and facet pathology, especially at L4-L5, sacroiliac joint, and other soft tissue foci. A small percentage of patients may have instability or foraminal stenosis, which eventually may require surgical intervention. The spondylolysis is not directly responsible for back pain in the majority of cases, evidenced by the fact that most patients respond to conservative treatment without bracing or invasive measures.
Many athletes with spondylolisthesis compete in rigorous sports without any reduction in their performance or any greater prevalence of back pain, when compared with controls. (1,2) Therefore, participation in sports should not be discouraged unless the pars defect is active or there is biomechanical instability, spinal stenosis, or neurologic deficit. The use of pre-employment low back radiographs as a screening measure to detect spondylolisthesis for industry is antiquated. In addition, the AMA’s Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment have been updated. (3) In 2000 the whole person impairment (7-9%) was established and correlated with pertinent clinical findings; the classification is much more realistic than the earlier inflated rates.
Hyland (4,5) offered a problem-based classification for spondylolisthesis that summarizes key clinical information and management issues. This classification is founded on four problem-based clinical presentations. (Table 5-7)
Recent Spondylolytic Spondylolisthesis.
Recent spondylolytic spondylolisthesis is the result of a recent traumatic or stress fracture and is usually painful and frequently debilitating. It is most commonly seen in adolescents who are physically active, especially in sports requiring repetitive hyperextension of the lumbar spine. These activities include, but are not limited to, diving, gymnastics, and pole vaulting, as well as sports that exert heavy stress to the lower back, such as weightlifting, football, and basketball. Recent spondylolytic spondylolisthesis has also been linked to inherited weakness (6) and biomechanical factors such as high sacral base angle (7) and increased disc angles (8), which appear to predispose a child for developing a defect in the pars interarticularis.
Pre-Existing Spondylolytic Spondylolisthesis: Stable.
Stable pre-existing spondylolytic spondylolisthesis is the end result of recent spondylolytic spondylolisthesis that has gone on to non-union and a permanent fibrous defect in the area of the pars interarticularis. Further progression of slippage is not expected after the age of 18, and most recent studies show no increase in frequency of low back pain or disability. (4,5) When spinal symptoms are present, a thorough search beyond the spondylolisthesis for altered biomechanics and alternate sources of pain is required. Pre-existing inactive spondylolytic spondylolisthesis is the most common type of spondylolisthesis seen in chiropractic clinical practice, and patients with the mere radiographic evidence of spondylolysis or spondylolisthesis who present with back pain often show excellent response to conservative chiropractic care. It is recommended that clinicians look past spondylolisthesis for the true cause of back pain in these patients.
Pre-Existing Spondylolytic Spondylolisthesis: Unstable.
Unstable pre-existing spondylolytic spondylolisthesis demonstrates incomplete fibrous union with resulting hypermobility and biomechanical instability at the spondylolisthetic level. It is uncommon, although no study has yet provided a good estimate of true incidence. This type may be the cause of recurrent episodes of back pain and prolonged disability in an adult or adolescent with progressive slippage. Instability is best determined radiographically but SPECT bone scans may also be of assistance.
Degenerative Spondylolisthesis.
Degenerative spondylolisthesis is found primarily in patients beyond the age of 40 and is much more common in women. It is estimated that approximately 25% of spondylolisthesis cases are the result of degenerative spondylolisthesis (pseudo-spondylolisthesis). As the percent of the population over age 50 increases, this proportion will also increase.
Unlike the other types, which are spondylolytic and frequently affect L5, degenerative spondylolisthesis does not have a pars defect and occurs much more frequently at L4. (9) The forward displacement occurs secondary to disc degeneration and facet joint arthrosis and often develops later in life. The predisposing factor is believed to be a straight and stable lumbosacral joint that sits higher than normal between the ilia. This arrangement, which may include sacralization of L5, puts increased stress on the L4-L5 intervertebral joint. This can lead to segmental hypermobility, ligament laxity, disc degeneration, and multiple small microfractures of the inferior articular processes of L4.
The amount of slippage that occurs in degenerative spondylolisthesis cases seldom exceeds 25% and is usually in the range of 10-15%. Foraminal compromise and/or lateral recess stenosis that results in neurological signs and symptoms is possibly the result of degenerative facet arthropathy. This may lead to the lateral recess or lateral nerve root entrapment syndrome, with patients having intermittent neurogenic claudication.

Medicolegal Implications
SPONDYLOLYSIS AND/OR SPONDYLOLISTHESIS
  • It is important to document the presence of unilateral or bilateral pars defects as it may relate to the degree of displacement or potential for displacement. Complete radiographic assessment to adequately demonstrate this information is considered essential.
  • Evaluation with kinematic flexion-extension or traction-compression radiography along with SPECT bone scans is essential in the overall assessment of patients with unstable spondylolisthesis. Often patients may have a history of significant trauma precipitating symptoms of lower back pain. Plain film radiography and CT do not provide physiologic information concerning the pars defect. SPECT may assist in determining whether this defect is a source of pain and whether the recent trauma has produced symptoms, aggravating the pre-existing spondylolysis and/or spondylolisthesis.
  • It is important to establish the cause of anterolisthesis in the cervical spine in every patient. In the presence of cervical spondylolisthesis, associated dysplasia of the neural arch frequently tips off the clinician to the underlying congenital cause. Approximately 50% of patients with cervical spondylolisthesis have an associated spina bifida occulta deformity. This is an important radiographic sign that should alert the clinician that this is not an acute fracture-dislocation. Cervical spondylolisthesis is rarely unstable on flexion-extension radiography, although these films should be performed as part of the evaluation.
  • Inappropriate spinal manipulation, which may worsen the patient’s symptoms, is contraindicated in patients with spondylolisthesis. An example of such inappropriate manipulation is prone recoil adjustments directly over the spondylolisthesis.
  • Manipulative treatment of type II spondylolytic patients can be instituted once contraindications have been excluded. Patients who demonstrate radiographic instability of lumbar spondylolisthesis by means of upright flexion-extension or compression-traction stress radiographs and positive SPECT scans should be referred for orthopedic or neurological consultation as part of the overall management of the unstable spondylolisthetic segment.
CAPSULE SUMMARY Spondylolysis and Spondylolisthesis
Definitions
  • Spondylolysis is an interruption of the pars interarticularis and may be either unilateral or bilateral. It is derived from two Greek roots, spondylos, meaning “vertebra,” and lysis, meaning a “dissolution or a loosing, setting free, releasing.”
  • Spondylolisthesis is an anterior displacement of a vertebral body in relationship to the segment immediately below. The Greek origins are from spondylos, meaning “vertebra,” and olisthesis, referring to “displacement.” Spondylolisthesis may be associated with defects in the pars interarticularis (spondylolytic spondylolisthesis) but may occur without them (non-spondylolytic spondylolisthesis).
  • Prespondylolisthesis is defined as the presence of spondylolysis without forward vertebral displacement. This term is misleading and should be discarded because it implies that displacement will necessarily occur.
General Considerations
  • The first recorded article relating to spondylolisthesis was in 1782 by the Belgian obstetrician Herbinaux.
Cause
  • The causes are varied and summarized by the Wiltse et al. classification:
    • Type I (dysplastic). A congenital abnormality in the upper sacrum or the neural arch of L5, allowing displacement to occur.
    • Type II (isthmic). A, A lytic or fatigue fracture of the pars; B, elongated but intact pars; C, acute fracture of the pars.
    • Type III (degenerative). Secondary to long-standing degenerative arthrosis of the zygapophyseal joints and discovertebral articulation.
    • Type IV (traumatic). Secondary to fractures in the area of the neural arch other than the pars interarticularis.
    • Type V (pathologic). In conjunction with generalized or localized bone disease (e.g., Paget’s disease, metastatic bone disease, osteopetrosis).
    • Type VI (iatrogenic). Occurs above or below a spinal fusion.
Incidence
  • The most common form is type IIA. In this type there may be an inherited predisposition coupled with increased biomechanical stresses and a defective healing response to produce the lesion. Stress fracture of the pars is the most common stress fracture in the body. Up to 7% of whites are found to have isthmic spondylolisthesis. Higher occurrences occur in athletes and Alaskan Eskimos.
Clinical Features
  • A very large percentage (estimated at 50%) of patients with isthmic spondylolisthesis never develop back pain related to the pars defects.
  • Those patients who suffer back pain usually have a separate biomechanical or pathologic cause for their symptoms, especially at the L4-L5 level.
  • Progression of displacement of type II spondylolistheses seldom occurs after the age of 18 years. It is thought by many that by the age of 10 years the greatest degree of displacement has been obtained.
  • With the exception of neurologic signs, physical examination findings in patients with isthmic spondylolisthesis depend on the degree of slip.
  • Patients with grades 1-2 may be normal on examination. Subtle signs may include prominent, heart-shaped buttocks,

    hamstring tightness, hyperlordosis of the lumbar spine, and a palpable step defect of the spinous process—a mobile spinous process often with crepitus (rattler). The stork test may indicate an active spondylolytic defect or facet syndrome.
  • Patients with grades 3 or greater have a symmetric transverse skin furrow above the iliac crests, hyperlordosis, and a posterior tilted pelvis and hip flexion (which produce a characteristic waddling gait). Hamstring tightness is prominent.
  • Neurological examination may be normal, but careful examination of the L5 nerve root (e.g., extensor hallucis longus) is critical to management and correlating imaging findings.
  • Many athletes with isthmic spondylolisthesis compete in vigorous sports without any reduction in their performance or any greater prevalence of back pain, as compared with controls. Participation in sports should not be discouraged unless biomechanical instability, spinal stenosis, or neurologic deficit is present.
  • Degenerative spondylolisthesis should be suspected in patients with neurogenic claudication.
  • Hyland has offered a problem-based clinical classification of spondylolisthesis: (a) recent spondylolytic spondylolisthesis, (b) pre-existing spondylolytic spondylolisthesis: stable, (c) pre-existing spondylolytic spondylolisthesis: unstable, and (d) degenerative spondylolisthesis.
Imaging Features
Plain Film Imaging
  • AP and lateral studies should be performed upright.
  • AP: Bowline of Brailsford, inverted Napoleon’s hat sign (Gendarme’s cap) (grade 3 or greater), scoliosis.
  • AP Tilt (Ferguson/Hibbs): Pars defect.
  • Lateral: Measurements—Meyerding’s grading (1,2,3,4), percentage slip, Ullmann’s line, slip angle, trapezoidal-shaped L5 (vertebral body index). Sacral doming, pars defect, L4 retrolisthesis, disc heights, sacral buttressing.
  • Oblique: Scotty dog analysis—collar sign, stepladder sign
  • Stress Views: Flexion-extension, compression-traction—slip measurements applied.
Nuclear Medicine
  • Planar scanning must be supplemented with SPECT study to depict active pars defects in isthmic spondylolisthesis.
  • SPECT: Tomographic method to obtain sectional and multiplanar images of the spine and shows active defects as localized increased uptake and provides evidence that the defect is the source of pain. Can allow assessment of response to treatment, especially with bracing. Also useful in postoperative assessment of pseudo-arthrosis, pars defect (spondylolysis acquisita), and infection.
CT
  • Provides the best detail on the pars defect and is able to show the cleft through the sclerosis and the presence of fragments. Can also assess nerve root compression and disc herniation but not the internal structural changes of disc pathology. Multislice technology allows high-detail multiplanar and three-dimensional images and can assist in the assessment of previously implanted hardware constructs.
MRI
  • Depiction of the defect can be made with low signal on T1-and T2-weighted studies. An active defect with bone marrow edema can be seen as bright signal on T2-weighted and STIR sequences. Assessment of disc integrity, especially L4-L5 and foraminal stenosis with nerve compression, is best assessed with MRI.
Treatment and Prognosis
  • Conservative management of isthmic spondylolisthesis includes activity modification, strengthening exercises for abdominal and back muscles, bedrest, low back antilordotic (Boston overlap) bracing, and selective non-narcotic analgesics. Different types of chiropractic therapy have been advocated. SPECT and MRI can be used to guide the response to therapy when the studies are initially positive.
  • Surgical intervention may sometimes be contemplated. Indicators include (a) unstable or progressive spondylolisthesis; (b) nerve compression syndromes; (c) failed conservative management; and (d) a child < 10 years who has a grade 2 or greater slip, features of a dysplastic spondylolisthesis, and progressive slip. Three general categories of surgery are performed: fusion, decompression, and a combination of both. Essentially, fusion is performed when instability or progressive slip has been documented and decompression when nerve root involvement is determined. Asymptomatic spondylolistheses usually do not need surgery.
Unilateral Isthmic Spondylolysis
  • Less common than the bilateral form and frequently overlooked on imaging. May be found in unilaterally loading activities and may progress to bilateral defects or heal.
Imaging Features
  • Usually, the diagnosis is suggested on plain film studies.
  • The key sign is a contralateral sclerotic pedicle Wilkinson’s syndrome, which may regress once the defect occurs bilaterally. Differential diagnosis includes osteoid osteoma, osteoblastoma, osteoblastic metastasis, and contralateral pedicle agenesis.
  • Other posterior arch changes include spinous process tilt, spina bifida occulta, facet dysplasia, laminal sclerosis, and fragmentation.
Cervical Spondylolisthesis
  • A congenital defect in the articular pillar, most commonly at C6 and less commonly at C2, C5, and C7. The pillar defect is nearly always bilateral and affects males 3:1. The majority of these lesions are asymptomatic and require no specific treatment.
Imaging Features
  • Imaging findings include a pillar cleft, pillar hypoplasia and dysplasia, pedicle agenesis, and in 50% of cases at C6, a spina bifida occulta. A small amount of anterolisthesis is usually present. Flexion-extension radiographs of the cervical spine are recommended to evaluate for motion segment instability.
  • CT and MRI may be indicated only when the diagnosis is not certain or in the presence of instability or neurological symptoms.
